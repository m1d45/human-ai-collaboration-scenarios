# FAQ

## What is this project?

This project is an open framework for describing **32 scenarios of human–AI collaboration** in the creative industries.

It helps answer a practical question:

> What kind of relationship between a human and an AI system is appropriate for a specific task?

The framework is not centered on model capability alone.  
It focuses on task structure, human responsibility, authorship, judgement, and collaboration mode.

---

## Is this a theory, a heuristic, or a practical tool?

It is primarily a **practical heuristic framework**.

It can support:
- reflection
- workflow planning
- teaching
- research discussion

It is not a strict scientific model in the predictive sense, and it is not intended to function as a rigid decision engine.

---

## Why are there exactly 32 scenarios?

The framework combines:
- 4 binary task attributes
- 1 binary task-stage distinction

That gives:
- 2^4 = 16 task profiles
- 16 × 2 task stages = 32 scenarios

So the number 32 is not symbolic.  
It follows directly from the internal structure of the model.

---

## Why are the answers binary?

Each dimension is simplified to:
- high / low
- yes / no
- exploration / implementation

This is a deliberate design choice.

The goal is not to capture every nuance of reality, but to make the framework:
- easy to use
- easy to discuss in teams
- operational in real workflows
- stable across contexts

A more granular scoring system would look more precise, but would often create false precision.

---

## Does binary simplification distort reality?

Yes, to some extent.

Real tasks are more complex than binary categories.  
However, the framework intentionally accepts some loss of nuance in exchange for clarity and usability.

It is designed as a practical decision aid, not as a complete ontology of creative work.

---

## Can one task belong to more than one scenario?

Yes.

A task may:
- sit between two scenarios
- shift from one scenario to another over time
- be organised differently by different teams

The framework is not meant to eliminate ambiguity completely.  
It is meant to make ambiguity more structured and discussable.

---

## Can a workflow move across scenarios?

Yes — and this is normal.

For example, a project may begin with:
- cooperation during exploration,
then move into:
- augmentation during development,
and end with:
- automation during production or scaling

The framework is most useful when applied to **specific tasks or phases**, not to an entire project as one undifferentiated whole.

---

## What is the difference between automation, augmentation, and cooperation?

### Automation
AI performs a substantial part of the task with minimal human participation during execution.  
The human controls the system rather than each individual output.

### Augmentation
AI strengthens human work, but the human remains the main decision-maker.  
The AI helps, but the human still sets goals, applies judgement, and takes responsibility.

### Cooperation
AI becomes part of an iterative or dialogic loop.  
The human and AI jointly shape the process through exchange, alternatives, refinement, and response.

---

## Is cooperation always better than automation?

No.

One of the key ideas of the framework is that **no mode is universally superior**.

A mode is only appropriate relative to:
- the task
- the stage of work
- the need for expertise
- the need for speed
- the importance of personal involvement
- the cost of error

In some cases, cooperation is exactly right.  
In other cases, it is inefficient or even risky.  
The same is true of automation and augmentation.

---

## Is automation always less “human” or less valuable?

Not necessarily.

Automation may be the most appropriate and responsible choice in tasks where:
- repetition dominates
- output volume matters
- personal authorship is not essential
- the human contribution adds little value at the object level
- system-level control is more useful than item-level involvement

The framework does not treat automation as inferior by definition.

---

## What does “personal involvement” mean in this framework?

This dimension is not just about whether a person likes the task.  
It is about whether human participation is itself an important part of the value of the process and the outcome.

Personal involvement may matter if:
- the output requires authorial presence;
- the person must personally stand behind the decision;
- the task depends on subjective judgement;
- the act of doing the task has value in itself as a form of creative engagement, professional development, interest, or enjoyment.

So the dimension can include enjoyment, but it is not reducible to enjoyment alone.

---

## Is this framework about model capability?

No, not primarily.

It is possible for:
- a powerful model to be used in a narrowly controlled role
- a less capable system to be used in a high-agency collaborative role

The framework describes the **structure of collaboration**, not the benchmark performance of a model.

---

## Does the framework apply only to creative industries?

It was developed primarily with creative-industry contexts in mind:
- design
- media
- content production
- education
- art-related workflows

Some parts of the framework may also be useful in other knowledge-work domains, but that is not its original scope.

So the short answer is:
- **primarily yes**
- **potentially broader later**

---

## Can the framework be used in education?

Yes.

This is one of its most valuable uses.

It can help students and educators:
- discuss human–AI collaboration more precisely
- distinguish between modes of work
- understand where judgement matters
- learn to choose a mode rather than merely use a tool
- reflect on authorship and responsibility

---

## Can the framework be used in research?

Yes.

It can be useful as:
- an analytical lens
- a discussion framework
- a teaching framework
- a comparative structure for case analysis

However, it should not automatically be treated as a validated scientific instrument.  
If used in research, it should be presented as a conceptual and methodological framework rather than as a universal empirical taxonomy.

---

## Is this framework normative?

Only in a limited sense.

It does not say:
- what people must do
- what mode is morally superior
- what the “future of work” must look like

It does suggest that some collaboration modes are more appropriate than others in particular task conditions.

So it is practical and evaluative, but not prescriptive in an absolute sense.

---

## What is the main value of the framework?

Its main value is that it provides a structured language for thinking about human–AI work.

Instead of vague statements like:
- “AI should help here”
- “This feels too important for AI”
- “This could probably be automated”
- “We want a more creative process”

the framework encourages a more precise discussion:
- What kind of task is this?
- Where does judgement matter?
- Where does authorship matter?
- Where is speed the main driver?
- Where is the cost of error too high?
- Where should human presence remain central?

That shift in language is one of the central contributions of the project.

---

## Is this framework finished?

No.

It is an evolving framework.

The following parts remain open to revision:
- wording
- examples
- localisation
- methodological boundaries
- scenario clarity
- interface design

The framework is open to critique, refinement, and contribution.
