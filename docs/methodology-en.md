# Methodology

## Overview

This project proposes a practical framework for describing how humans and AI systems collaborate in the creative industries.

Its core claim is simple: the impact of generative AI should not be described only in terms of what models can do, but in terms of how responsibility, authorship, decision-making, and task structure are redistributed between people and AI systems.

The framework therefore does not classify models. It classifies **modes of collaboration**.

It is designed for use in:
- design practice
- media and content production
- education
- research
- creative direction
- organisational design of AI-supported workflows

---

## Why this framework exists

Generative AI does not only automate routine work. It enters parts of the creative process that were previously associated with taste, intuition, stylistic variation, rapid ideation, and early-stage concept generation.

What becomes cheaper is not “creativity” in the abstract, but a specific layer of creative labour:
drafts, variants, exploratory outputs, first versions, stylistic alternatives, reference collections, preliminary structures, and other intermediate forms.

When the cost of producing variants falls sharply, value shifts elsewhere:
- task framing
- selection
- judgement
- responsibility
- authorship
- verification

This is why the framework focuses on **how a task should be organised**, not on whether AI is “good” or “bad” for creative work.

---

## Three major collaboration modes

The framework distinguishes three major modes of human–AI collaboration.

### 1. Automation

In automation mode, AI performs a substantial part of the task with minimal human participation during execution.

The human role shifts upward:
- setting parameters
- defining templates
- auditing outputs
- controlling the system rather than each individual unit

Automation is most appropriate where:
- personal involvement is not essential
- the task is structured
- output volume matters
- repetitive execution creates little additional human value

Automation does **not** mean “AI works by itself.”  
It means human control has moved from the level of each item to the level of the system.

---

### 2. Augmentation

In augmentation mode, AI strengthens human work while the human remains the main decision-maker.

AI may help with:
- search
- summarisation
- comparison
- variation
- structuring
- drafting
- adaptation

But the human retains control over:
- goals
- criteria
- interpretation
- final judgement
- responsibility

Augmentation is especially useful when:
- human expertise matters
- the task benefits from speed
- the task still requires human evaluation
- the person must remain the primary author or accountable actor

---

### 3. Cooperation

In cooperation mode, AI becomes part of an iterative loop rather than a passive tool.

This means AI is not only executing instructions, but actively contributing to the movement of the process by:
- generating alternatives
- reframing the problem
- surfacing contradictions
- proposing unexpected directions
- supporting iteration under uncertainty

The human and AI work in a dialogic or co-creative relation.

Cooperation is most relevant when:
- personal involvement matters
- the task is exploratory or conceptually open
- non-obvious alternatives are valuable
- the process benefits from iterative exchange

This mode is powerful, but it also creates a specific risk:
the illusion of control. A fluent or persuasive output can be mistaken for a valid one.

---

## Why task structure matters more than model type

A central premise of this framework is that the same model can play very different roles depending on the task.

The key question is not:
> What can this model do?

The key question is:
> What kind of relationship between human and AI is appropriate for this task?

This is why the framework is built around task conditions rather than model features.

---

## The five dimensions of the framework

The classification is generated from five dimensions.

### 1. Task stage

Is the task primarily:
- **exploration**, or
- **implementation**?

Exploration includes:
- research
- discovery
- sense-making
- framing
- hypothesis generation
- concept search
- reference gathering

Implementation includes:
- execution
- refinement
- production
- formatting
- adaptation
- delivery
- finalisation

This distinction matters because AI behaves differently across these phases.  
In exploratory contexts it expands possibility space.  
In implementation contexts it supports convergence, structure, repetition, and production.

---

### 2. Expertise required

Does the task require specialised knowledge or professional judgement?

This dimension distinguishes between tasks where:
- AI output can be evaluated without deep domain knowledge, and
- tasks where expert evaluation is essential

The question is not whether expertise exists somewhere in the organisation.  
The question is whether **this task** depends on it.

---

### 3. Speed pressure

Does the task need to be completed quickly?

This dimension captures not abstract efficiency, but time pressure as an operational factor.

High speed pressure often changes which collaboration mode is viable:
- some tasks become more suitable for automation
- some require rapid augmentation
- some demand urgent cooperation under pressure

---

### 4. Personal involvement

Is it important that the person takes part in doing this task themselves, rather than simply receiving the result?

This dimension is not limited to efficiency alone.  
It describes whether human participation is itself an important part of the task’s value.

Personal involvement may matter because:
- the output requires an authorial presence;
- the person must personally stand behind the decision;
- the task depends on subjective judgement;
- the act of doing the task has value in itself — as a form of creative engagement, professional development, interest, or enjoyment.

This means the dimension captures not only the value of the outcome, but also the value of human participation in the process.

---

### 5. Cost of error

Would an error in this task be expensive, risky, or difficult to correct?

This includes:
- financial cost
- reputational cost
- legal cost
- organisational risk
- strategic risk
- public consequences

This dimension affects how much validation and human oversight are necessary.

---

## Why the framework uses binary values

Each dimension is treated as binary:
- high / low
- yes / no
- exploration / implementation

This does not imply that reality is binary.

The framework uses binary distinctions because the goal is not theoretical exhaustiveness, but operational usefulness.

Fine-grained scales often create false precision:
- what exactly is “expertise = 7/10”?
- who assigns the score?
- how stable is it across teams?

Binary distinctions reduce cognitive noise and help teams reach faster alignment.

The framework is therefore intentionally simplified in order to be usable in real work settings.

---

## Why there are 32 scenarios

The framework combines:
- 4 binary task attributes
- 1 binary task stage

This gives:
- 2^4 = 16 task profiles
- 16 × 2 stages = 32 scenarios

Each scenario represents a distinct configuration of:
- human role
- AI role
- appropriate collaboration mode
- typical risks
- typical use cases

The number 32 is not symbolic.  
It is the result of the combinatorial structure of the model.

---

## What the framework is for

This framework can be used in at least four ways.

### 1. Reflective use
To analyse how a person or team is already working with AI.

### 2. Planning use
To choose an appropriate collaboration mode before starting a task.

### 3. Educational use
To teach students and practitioners how to think about AI not only as a tool, but as a changing structure of work.

### 4. Research use
To analyse human–AI collaboration in terms of authorship, delegation, judgement, responsibility, and process design.

---

## What the framework is not

This framework is not:
- a universal theory of creativity
- a ranking of AI systems
- a prediction engine
- a prescription that one mode is always better than another
- a complete description of every real workflow

It is a **heuristic decision framework**.

Its purpose is not to eliminate ambiguity, but to make ambiguity more structured and discussable.

---

## Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged.

### 1. Real workflows can move across scenarios
A task may begin in one scenario and end in another.

### 2. One task may support more than one valid mode
Different teams may organise the same task differently.

### 3. Binary simplification reduces nuance
This is a deliberate trade-off for usability.

### 4. The framework describes collaboration structure, not model capability
It is possible for a powerful model to still be used in a low-agency role, or vice versa.

### 5. The framework is currently grounded in creative-industry contexts
It may be adaptable to other domains, but it was not built from those contexts first.

---

## Practical interpretation

The framework is most useful when treated not as a rigid classifier, but as a structured conversation device.

Its value lies in helping people ask better questions:
- What exactly is this task?
- Where does judgement matter?
- Where does authorship matter?
- Where can speed legitimately dominate?
- Where is the cost of error too high for delegation?
- Where is human presence necessary?
- Where is it merely habitual?

These questions are often more important than the final label itself.

---

## Final principle

The main practical principle of the framework is:

> Do not decide how to use AI before you have described the task.

The value of the model lies in making that description systematic.

It helps replace vague intuitions like:
- “AI should help here”
- “AI should not touch this”
- “This feels too important”
- “This could probably be automated”

with a clearer structure for reasoning and discussion.

That structure is the real contribution of the framework.
